Marshmallow Test

At this point, you might be wondering what the point of that poorly designed waiting game was about. That was a replica of a well-known test called the Marshmallow Test created by Walter Mischel and Ebbe B. Ebbesen at Stanford University in 1960. The Marshmallow Test is usually conducted on children between the ages of 3 and 5, and the procedure consists of leaving a child alone in a room with a single marshmallow in front of him/her. The child is then told that he/she can eat the marshmallow right now or wait a couple of minutes and the experimenter will bring an additional marshmallow for the child to enjoy. The study showed that about two-thirds of the children either ate the marshmallow right away or took a couple of minutes to think it over and then ate it. On the other hand, the other third of the children waited for the second marshmallow which the experimenter brought in 15 minutes later.

This test studied if having the ability to delay instant lesser rewards has any correlation with the person's ability to become successful. It turns out that there is a significant difference between those who waited and those who did not. In fact, the experimenters surveyed the children once they were in high school and found out that those who waited for the second marshmallow had higher grades, were less stressed out, got accepted to better colleges, and were, in general, better off than those who did not wait for the second marshmallow.

For example, if you look at the charts below, the chart on the left shows the Body Mass Index (BMI) differences between the Non-Delayers (the children who did not wait for the second marshmallow) and the Delayers (the children who waited for the second marshmallow) recorded as a follow-up study years after the subjects were tested as children. It is clear to see that the average BMI differs by about 3 points which is a significant difference. In fact, according to World Health Organization (WHO), 27.5 (Non-Delayers) is considered Overweight and 24.4 (Delayers) is considered Normal/Healthy.

On the other hand, the chart on the right shows the Scholastic Aptitude/Assessment Test (SAT) scores between the Non-Delayers and Delayers. There is about 210 points difference in the total score which adds onto the significance between the difference in Non-Delayers and Delayers.

These two charts, however, are only a part of a large follow-up studies conducted by multiple psychologists throughout the decades. There are other results such as divorce rate, stress level, etc. that demonstrate the better quality of life that the Delayers are experiencing compared to that of the Non-Delayers. So what is it about not eating a marshmallow right away that makes someone successful? The main factor that the Delayers have is the ability to delay instant gratification. In fact, the ability to delay instant gratification is important in that those who don't have this ability are victims of an international epidemic: procrastiation.

Dangers

We all know what procrstaintion is but what exactly is the definition of procrastiation? Experts define it as the voluntary delay of some task that we intend to do, despite knowing that we will suffer as a result. The key word here is suffer. Procrastination is dangerous as many people will agree with. In fact, it is very easy for a person to become a chronic procrastinator, or someone who procrastinates frequently and consistently. About 20% of adults are considered chronic procrastinators and it is extremelty difficult to break out of the procrastiantion cycle.

Both the ease of becoming a chronic procrastinator and the difficulty of breaking out of the procrastination cycle stem from the fact that the minds of human tend to reconstruct the sitatuions to make themselves feel beter. For example, let's say Tom is a college student who didn't study for an exam until the night before. Because he didn't have enough time to study for the exam, he recieves a solid C on the exam. Now, unless you don't really mind your GPA, C on an exam is a pretty bad score that will bring down your grade quite a bit if you're aiming for an A. Instead of thinking about the poor work habit, Tom thinks to himself, 'I could've done worse' or 'This is not bad considering I studied the night before.' This process is called downward counterfactual. Downward counterfactual thinking focuses on how the situation could have been worse. This is a common thinking process that many people go through (I'm also guilty of this). By thinking in this way, Tom is not able to focus on how he could have done better in the exam and improve his work habit; instead, by the time the next exam rolls around, he'll think to himeself that he only has to put a little bit more work since he got away with a "respectable" C when he barely studied.

There are other excuses that people make to feel better about themselves. Many people will say that they work better under pressure or even say that as lnog as the work gets done, it's fine. People reconstruct situations, whether it be consciously or unconsciously, so that they always accomplish "something". As a matter of fact, procrastiation is the absence of progress; recreating the unhealthy situation into something that makes the person temporarily feel good will prevent the person from procrastinating in the future and also add another person into the large pool of chronic procrastinators in the world.
