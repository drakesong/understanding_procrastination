Marshmallow Test

At this point, you might be wondering what the point of that poorly designed waiting game was about. That was a replica of a well-known test called the Marshmallow Test created by Walter Mischel and Ebbe B. Ebbesen at Stanford University in 1960. The Marshmallow Test is usually conducted on children between the ages of 3 and 5, and the procedure consists of leaving a child alone in a room with a single marshmallow in front of him/her. The child is then told that he/she can eat the marshmallow right now or wait a couple of minutes and the experimenter will bring an additional marshmallow for the child to enjoy. The study showed that about two-thirds of the children either ate the marshmallow right away or took a couple of minutes to think it over and then ate it. On the other hand, the other third of the children waited for the second marshmallow which the experimenter brought in 15 minutes later.

This test studied if having the ability to delay instant lesser rewards has any correlation with the person's ability to become successful. It turns out that there is a significant difference between those who waited and those who did not. In fact, the experimenters surveyed the children once they were in high school and found out that those who waited for the second marshmallow had higher grades, were less stressed out, got accepted to better colleges, and were, in general, better off than those who did not wait for the second marshmallow.

For example, if you look at the charts below, the chart on the left shows the Body Mass Index (BMI) differences between the Non-Delayers (the children who did not wait for the second marshmallow) and the Delayers (the children who waited for the second marshmallow) recorded as a follow-up study years after the subjects were tested as children. It is clear to see that the average BMI differs by about 3 points which is a significant difference. In fact, according to World Health Organization (WHO), 27.5 (Non-Delayers) is considered Overweight and 24.4 (Delayers) is considered Normal/Healthy.

On the other hand, the chart on the right shows the Scholastic Aptitude/Assessment Test (SAT) scores between the Non-Delayers and Delayers. There is about 210 points difference in the total score which adds onto the significance between the difference in Non-Delayers and Delayers.

These two charts, however, are only a part of a large follow-up studies conducted by multiple psychologists throughout the decades. There are other results such as divorce rate, stress level, etc. that demonstrate the better quality of life that the Delayers are experiencing compared to that of the Non-Delayers. So what is it about not eating a marshmallow right away that makes someone successful? The main factor that the Delayers have is the ability to delay instant gratification. In fact, the ability to delay instant gratification is important in that those who don't have this ability are victims of an international epidemic: procrastiation.

Dangers

We all know what procrstaintion is but what exactly is the definition of procrastiation? Experts define it as the voluntary delay of some task that we intend to do, despite knowing that we will suffer as a result. The key word here is suffer. Procrastination is dangerous as many people will agree with. In fact, it is very easy for a person to become a chronic procrastinator, or someone who procrastinates frequently and consistently. About 20% of adults are considered chronic procrastinators and it is extremelty difficult to break out of the procrastiantion cycle.

Both the ease of becoming a chronic procrastinator and the difficulty of breaking out of the procrastination cycle stem from the fact that the minds of human tend to reconstruct the sitatuions to make themselves feel beter. For example, let's say Tom is a college student who didn't study for an exam until the night before. Because he didn't have enough time to study for the exam, he recieves a solid C on the exam. Now, unless you don't really mind your GPA, C on an exam is a pretty bad score that will bring down your grade quite a bit if you're aiming for an A. Instead of thinking about the poor work habit, Tom thinks to himself, 'I could've done worse' or 'This is not bad considering I studied the night before.' This process is called downward counterfactual. Downward counterfactual thinking focuses on how the situation could have been worse. This is a common thinking process that many people go through (I'm also guilty of this). By thinking in this way, Tom is not able to focus on how he could have done better in the exam and improve his work habit; instead, by the time the next exam rolls around, he'll think to himeself that he only has to put a little bit more work since he got away with a "respectable" C when he barely studied.

There are other excuses that people make to feel better about themselves. Many people will say that they work better under pressure or even say that as lnog as the work gets done, it's fine. People reconstruct situations, whether it be consciously or unconsciously, so that they always accomplish "something". As a matter of fact, procrastiation is the absence of progress; recreating the unhealthy situation into something that makes the person temporarily feel good will prevent the person from procrastinating in the future and also add another person into the large pool of chronic procrastinators in the world.

Process

Now that we understand the dangers of procrastination, we need to understand how it works so that we can not only recognize when we're procrastinating but also use different countering techniques to break out of the procrastination cycle.

There are many factors that play into procrastination. We will start with the most basic ones: the type of task at hand.

Task

Frustration

The first factor that plays into what kind of task at hand is the frustration level of the task. This frustration can be caused by many reasons such as the task being too difficult, too large, etc. Being frustrated makes one more difficult to focus on a task because frustration causes uncontrollable cognitions and task-irrelevant emotions. In order to manage these frustrations, one is more likely to procrastinate by distracting oneself with other non-frustrating activities.

Below shows an average person, let's name him Joe, working on three different tasks: not frustration (left), mildly frustrating (middle), and very frustration (right). You can compare the difference in task-completion time by looking at the time (all three start at the same time) and the Work Done counter. As before, the task at hand is moving the rectangular block from left to right. Although, Joe moves the block in a linear fashion, imagine that he moves it a little bit at a time, gets distracted by something, resumes working, and such as you saw above.

It is clear to see that the higher frustration is, the longer it takes to copmlete the task which, in this case, represents Joe procrastinating while working. Frustration is only one factor out of many however.

Personal Meaning

Another factor related to the task at hand that adds onto the procrastination level is personal meaning. Personal meaning is the extent to which one feels the task at hand is worthwhile pursuit. This includes components of the feature such as fun, pleasure, passion, etc. The lower the personal meaning, the less desirable it is to do the task, which leads to more procrastination.

Below you'll see Joe again with three different tasks: low frustration + high personal meaning (left), average frustration + average personal meaning (center), and high frustration + low personal meaning.

Compared to the figure above, it is clear to see that Joe completes the task that is both not frustrating and has high personal meaning versus the task that is only not frustrating. The other extreme is also true; the task that is both very frustrating and has no personal meaning takes a longer time to complete compared to the task that is only very frustrating. With this, we can clearly see that there are many factors that are playing a role in determining the type of task that is at hand.

We will now go over all of the other factors that play a role in procrastination.

Boredom

Similar to frustration, the more boring the task is, the more likely the person is going to deviate towards a task that is less boring. Boring tasks also tend to cause drowsiness and sleepiness which impairs one's ability to work by a great deal.

Structure

Structure of the task means how well the task is organized and work through. When a task is less structured and difficult to coordinate, there is more procrastination. This is due to the fact that when one works through a task, there are many points during the process when the person has to make a decision about what to do next and what the next steps are to complete the task. If a task is clearly structured, it is easy for the person to decide what the next steps are and complete the task. On the other hand, if the task is not clearly organized, the indecision between the steps creates more chances for the person to procrastinate.

Delay

Delay represents the deadline of the task. Tasks that are due further away in time have lower priorities for people. This is related to the temporal discounting theory: people discount rewards taht are further away. The higher the delay, the more procrastination there is. Delay is a dangerous factor in that there are tasks that have no deadlines. For example, many people for their new year's resolution decide to go on a diet by eating less and going out to the gym more. This is a task that has no deadline; it is a personal task that won't be completed until the person is satisified with the progress. When a task has no deadline, procrastination is very difficult to determine since the mathematically doesn't work out anymore (the variable representing delay becomes infinity). It is purely up to the person to motivate oneself to complete the task.

Personality

The task is not the only thing that affects procrastination, however. The more important factor is the personality of the person. No matter how structured and how easy the task is, if the person is literally a sloth, the task is not going to get done anytime soon. Let's start exploring what kind of personality traits play a role in procrastination.

Conscientiousness

A conscientious person is who wishes to what is right, especially to do one's work or duty well and thoroughly. One of the elements of conscientiousness is organization and this results in good coping strategies such as goal setting and automatic habits. Achievement motivation, enjoying performance for the sake of performance, is also an iporant aspect of conscientiousness. High conscientiousness results in less procrastination.

Below is a figure that shows three different types of people: very conscientious (left), average (center), not conscientious (right). Numerical values that is used to represent procrastination is also displayed. The model will be presented later in the paper but the numbers for now should be helpful when comparing procrastination.

Impulsiveness

As seen with the type of tasks, there are multiple personality traits that play a role in procrastination. Another personality factor that we'll see is impulsiveness. Impulsive people are more likely to act on the desire of the moment. When some distractions come along that are more interesting than what they should be doing, they are likely to switch to the more interesting activitiy. This leads to procrastination.

Below there are three workers: very conscientious + low impulsiveness (left), average conscientious + average impulsiveness (center), low conscientious + high impulsiveness (right).

The left and the right are very unlikely scenarios as you can see how fast the person completes the task on the left and how slow the person takes on the right. This is due the fact that compared to the extreme cases for tasks, personality traits play a bigger role in determining the procrastination.

Sensation-Seeking

This is the tendency to find exciting activities, take risks and avoid boredom. The higher someone scores on this, the higher the procrastination.

Self-Esteem

This is how one evaluates oneself. A low self-evaluation correlates with automatic thoughts about oneself and procrastinatory cognitions, which leads to more procrastination.

Temptations

The last category of factors that affect procrastination is temptations. If there are no temptations (i.e. distractions) during the process of completing the task, then procrastination is likely to be low because there are less chances of distracting oneself.

Intensity

THe intensity of temptations can be related to the number of temptations and the difficulty of rejecting the temptations. The higher the intensity, the more likely the person is to procrastinate.
